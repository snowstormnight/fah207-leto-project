## Public Position

### Food is not a background issue. It is the beginning of everything.

Cities, governments, armies, and families all depend on a steady food supply. Demeter’s power may not be as dramatic as thunder or war, but it is more basic. She controls the harvest, and without the harvest, nothing else can continue.

## Campaign Pitch

# Demeter keeps civilization alive.

## **NO HARVEST. NO FUTURE.**

Demeter’s case is simple: every society depends on the land. People can build monuments, create laws, and fight wars, but none of that matters if crops fail. Her power is not based on spectacle. It is based on necessity.

Symbols

Symbols and Powers

# Symbols and Powers

## Tab 1: Grain

**Quote: “Every city begins in a field.”**

Grain is Demeter’s most recognizable symbol because it connects her directly to food and survival. In Greek and Roman art, wheat often appears in her hand or beside her body. It helps viewers identify her, but it also represents much more: farming, storage, trade, and the ability of a city to feed its people.

A ruler may control an empire, but Demeter controls whether that empire has enough to eat.

---

## Tab 2: Torch

**Quote: “She kept searching when everyone else looked away.”**

Demeter carries torches while searching for Persephone after she is taken to the underworld. The torch represents her refusal to stop searching, but it also connects her to ritual and mystery.

It brings light into darkness and suggests movement between different worlds: life and death, the surface and the underworld, loss and return. The torch makes Demeter more than an agricultural goddess. It shows her as a figure of endurance.

---

## Tab 3: The Seasons

**Quote: “Renewal comes with conditions.”**

The story of Demeter and Persephone explains the seasonal cycle through human emotion. When Persephone is gone, Demeter refuses to let the earth grow. When Persephone returns, fertility returns with her.

This makes Demeter both caring and dangerous. She can give abundance, but she can also take it away. Her power depends on balance, and when that balance is broken, everyone suffers.

# Evidence Exhibits

## Exhibit 01

### Demeter and Persephone from the East Pediment of the Parthenon

**Date:** c. 438–432 BCE
**Medium:** Pentelic marble
**Image:** Two seated female figures, usually identified as Demeter and Persephone

### **Even Athena’s Temple Needed Demeter**

These two figures were placed in the east pediment of the Parthenon, which showed the birth of Athena. Their identities are not completely certain, but they are often identified as Demeter and Persephone.

The figures sit closely together. One rests her arm across the other, making the group feel calm and personal. Their clothing is carved with deep folds across the legs, while thinner folds follow the shapes of the bodies underneath. This is typical of High Classical sculpture, where the body remains visible even beneath heavy drapery.

Their position matters. The Parthenon celebrated Athena and the power of Athens, but Demeter and Persephone still appear within that divine gathering. Their presence suggests that civic strength also depended on fertility, agriculture, and seasonal renewal.

Athens could celebrate military victory, but it still needed grain.

---

## Exhibit 02

### Demeter on the East Frieze of the Parthenon

**Date:** c. 447–438 BCE
**Medium:** Pentelic marble, low relief
**Image:** Demeter seated with Hermes, Dionysos, and Ares

### **She Belonged at the Centre of Civic Life**

The Parthenon frieze shows the Panathenaic procession, one of the major public festivals of Athens. Near the centre, the gods sit and watch as the human procession approaches.

Demeter appears among Hermes, Dionysos, and Ares. She does not need a dramatic pose. Her size, calm posture, and position among the gods already establish her importance.

The relief is shallow, but overlapping bodies and carefully arranged drapery create a sense of depth. The gods are seated so they can fit inside the narrow space while still appearing larger than the human participants.

This image places Demeter inside the public identity of Athens. She is not shown as a distant rural goddess. She is present in the symbolic centre of the city and its religious life.

---

## Exhibit 03

### The Abduction of Persephone, Tomb of Persephone at Vergina

**Date:** c. 340 BCE
**Medium:** Wall painting
**Image:** Hades carrying Persephone away in a chariot

### **One Abduction Changed the Entire World**

Demeter does not appear in this painting, but the event shown here causes her most important act of power.

Hades drives Persephone toward the underworld while she twists away from him. Her raised arms, turned body, and moving drapery make the scene feel sudden and violent. The chariot and horses pull the eye across the wall, making the viewer feel the speed of the abduction.

Unlike the calm balance of Classical sculpture, this painting depends on emotion and movement. The lines are quick, the poses are unstable, and the figures seem caught in the middle of an event.

After Persephone is taken, Demeter stops the earth from producing crops. Her personal grief becomes a crisis for all humanity. She forces the other gods to respond because people can no longer survive.

The painting shows the loss. Demeter’s reaction proves the scale of her power.

## Exhibit 04

### Terracotta Group, Possibly Demeter and Persephone

**Date:** Second–first century BCE
**Medium:** Terracotta
**Image:** Two seated women leaning closely toward one another

### **Her Power Was Also Personal**

This small terracotta group is often interpreted as Demeter and Persephone, although the identification is uncertain.

The figures sit close together, and their poses suggest conversation or emotional connection. One leans toward the other rather than facing forward in a formal pose. Their clothing is carefully layered, and the folds help separate the two bodies while also linking them visually.

The small size changes the way the object would have been viewed. Unlike a temple sculpture, it invites close and private attention. The relationship between the figures becomes more important than their divine status.

This fits the Hellenistic interest in emotion, personal experience, and informal moments. Demeter is not shown controlling nature. Instead, the image focuses on the bond between mother and daughter.

That relationship is still one of the strongest parts of her story.

---

## Exhibit 05

### Julia Domna as Ceres

**Date:** c. 203 CE
**Medium:** Marble
**Image:** Julia Domna shown in the form of Ceres

### **Rome Used Her Image to Look Stable**

The Romans knew Demeter as Ceres. Her image became especially useful in imperial portraiture because she represented fertility, abundance, and the welfare of the state.

In this statue, Julia Domna’s portrait is combined with the body and clothing of Ceres. Her hairstyle identifies her as the empress, while the goddess’s attributes connect her to agriculture and prosperity.

The result is both portrait and political message. Julia Domna appears as a real person, but she also takes on the timeless authority of a goddess.

The statue suggests that the imperial family could provide stability and abundance. This was important because Roman power did not depend only on conquest. Emperors also had to appear capable of protecting the food supply and maintaining social order.

Rome borrowed Demeter’s image because her authority was already understood.

# Comparison

## Demeter and Dionysos

Demeter and Dionysos are both connected to agriculture, seasonal growth, and ritual. However, they represent very different sides of cultivation.

Demeter represents the conditions needed for life. Dionysos represents celebration, wine, and release after the harvest.

---

## Side A: Demeter and Persephone

The Parthenon pediment group is calm and stable. The figures sit close together, and the heavy drapery gives them a strong physical presence.

The image focuses on continuity, relationship, and return. Demeter’s power is not shown through action. It is shown through stability.

---

## Side B: Triumph of Dionysos and the Seasons Sarcophagus

**Date:** c. 260–270 CE
**Medium:** Marble
**Image:** Dionysos surrounded by seasonal figures, animals, and followers

The sarcophagus is crowded with movement. Figures overlap, bodies twist, and deep carving creates strong shadows. The viewer has very little empty space in which to rest.

The imagery presents abundance as excess. Dionysos is surrounded by celebration, animals, fruit, and members of his procession. The four Seasons appear as human figures, turning the passage of time into a display of fertility and wealth.

Dionysos represents what happens when the harvest succeeds.

Demeter decides whether it succeeds at all.

---

## Final Takeaway

Dionysos controls the celebration.

Demeter controls whether there is anything to celebrate.

Final Verdict

# Final Verdict

## Every meal is already part of Demeter’s campaign.

Modern life makes food look easy. It arrives in stores, restaurants, and packages, far away from the fields where it began.

But the basic dependence has not changed. People still rely on soil, weather, labour, and seasonal growth. Demeter remains relevant because civilization has never stopped depending on the things she represents.

Modern connection

Modern Connection

# Modern Connection

## Demeter never disappeared. Her image simply changed.

Demeter’s name still appears in modern agriculture. Demeter International uses her name for biodynamic certification, connecting the ancient goddess of grain with ideas about soil health, farming, and sustainability.

The choice works because her identity is still easy to understand. Demeter represents the relationship between people and the land. She reminds consumers that food does not simply appear. It comes from systems that can be protected, damaged, controlled, or exhausted.

Her myth also remains important in modern writing and psychology. The story of Demeter and Persephone is often used to explore motherhood, separation, grief, independence, and control.

Environmental writers can also use the myth to discuss climate change and ecological collapse. When Demeter withdraws fertility from the earth, the result is not symbolic. It is a food crisis.

Her story remains useful because it connects personal emotion with large social consequences. One mother’s grief becomes a problem for the entire world.
