Portrayal of the God Dionysus Across Vase Sculptures and Paintings: Why did he have a following, and should you join too?

The chaotic and ecstatic tale of Dionysus has been portrayed with the use of pottery and vase painting within the Hellenistic era (Kim, 2021). Being the god of wine, theatre, celebration, and madness, drinking cups and chalices were often symbols that were associated with Dionysus’ array of rituals and celebration (Kraemer, 1979, p.58). In Ancient Greek vase paintings, Dionysus is typically shown with his wand or staff; the Thyrsus, which shifted in style during the Hellenistic era as it was not depicted in black-figure vases prior to the fifth century (Olszewski, 2019, p.156). The Borghese Vase shows Dionysus in his usual nature, looking energetic as he dances, holding two thyrsus: one, appearing as an artichoke, in his right hand, and the other in a panther’s mouth (Olszewski, 2019, p.163). The artichoke, as well as other botanical appearances, appears significantly within the Hellenistic period in the form of Dionysus’ thyrsus (Olszewski, 2019, p.163).

As Dionysus is worshipped for a range of activities, from wine harvest productivity to a multitude of rituals, many red-figure vase paintings show him at the centre of the maenads: a cult of mad women obsessed with Dionysus (Kraemer, 1979, p.64). The ecstaticize of Dionysus and his maenads are portrayed on these vases as dancers in the process of worship, and are based upon reality as opposed to mythological figural depiction (Kraemer, 1979, p.64). The beginning of the Hellenistic period marks the beginning of inscriptions regarding Dionysiac cults as a means to incorporate and spread his worship (Kraemer, 1979, p.66). Their activities, being later portrayed on vase paintings, consisted of “dancing, snake-handling, and wandering on the mountains nocturnally” (Kraemer, 1979, p.66). The cult initiations of the maenads were seen on vase paintings from the participants dressed in fawnskins, showing the sacrificial ritual of the animal into the cult (Kraemer, 1979, p.71). Although Dionysus’ values could have been perceived as scandalous or controversial, in reality it gave women the ability to break free of domestic responsibilities and find freedom from the rigidity in social culture (Kraemer, 1979, p.79)

The Francois Vase, a black-figure vessel or crater from approximately 570 BCE, was found likely as a tomb offering, like much painted pottery of the time (Kim, 2021). This detailed vase is the host of many mythological inscriptions and figural depictions, specifically the ‘Return of Hephaistos’, which tells the Homeric Hymn to Dionysos (Hedreen, 2015, p.164). The Francois Vase depicts Dionysus, a controversial figure, and his entourage of “obscene processional movement” (Hedreen, 2015, p.166). This social hierarchy, shown in the scene of the Wedding of Peleus and Thetis, is evident, both in the fact that Dionysus travels in the precession on foot, and also with the ongoing clash in values between Olympian gods and society and Dionysus' “obscenity” (Hedreen, 2015, p.169). Other deities travel in horse-drawn chariots, while Dionysus walks hurriedly as he carries a large vase, showing his lower position in the hierarchical Olympos social classes (Hedreen, 2015, p.172). Dionysus’ position as the bearer of wine to the wedding, is a demonstration of his “gift to humankind” (Hedreen, 2015, p.174).

Dionysus has persisted in being a figure crucial to excitement, entertainment, and a long-lasting sense of chaos that appears to erupt in any space he enters. Although these qualities may not pertain attractive to everyone, it is undeniable the necessity of figures such as Dionysus to contribute towards the progression of cultures and society. Dionysus continues to have extreme relevance in the cultivation of theatre and plays, both through the telling of his own life as well as his enjoyment for drama, demonstrating an ongoing display of complex and intertwined human emotions (Massini, 2023, p.2). As Dionysus was known to be the lord of mysteries and inspiration, his existence transformed eventually into the Roman god Bacchus, humanized into the traits of drunkenness and wine, which many have related to throughout time (Massini, 2023, p.2). In modern history, Dionysus has served as inspiration for great authors and poets, such as Nietzsche; he spoke upon the dichotomy of Apollo and Dionysus,  which had also previously been study during the Antiquity and the Renaissance (Massini, 2023, p.8). Although the act of madness, exemplified through Dionysus, is typically regarded as a negative and shameful trait, he was able to personify such a trait and bring normalcy to the portrayal of mad figures, either in a frenzy from too much wine or manic from emotions (Massini, 2023, p.10). Dionysus was therefore able to set individuals free from the constraining conventions that have existed throughout time (Massini, 2023, p.10). Finally, Dionysian festivals became a prime space to prioritize the joint harmony of nature and unity between civilians, and was a space to enjoy music and dance and indulge in certain "obscenities" without the fear of repercussion (Massini, 2023, p.12). These qualities and way of living life without shame and emphasizing pleasure and connection in society were emphasized by Dionysus; his actions continue to be portrayed in cultures through the appreciation of entertainment, perhaps with a side of wine.
